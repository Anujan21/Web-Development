HISTORICAL PROGRESSION

First Black Hole Confirmed

Published December 2019

A black hole is a region in space that has characteristics that can seem very bizarre and unreal. They are extremely dense, and they have a gravitational acceleration that is so strong that nothing can escape from it when caught. Not even light, which can travel faster than anything else in the universe. Huge black holes are expected to form when very massive stars collapse at the end of their life cycle. After a black hole has formed, it can continue to grow by absorbing mass from its surroundings. By absorbing other stars and merging with other black holes, supermassive black holes of millions of solar masses may form. Most scientists agree that in the center of most galaxies, there are supermassive black holes that exist. Albert Einstein first predicted the existence of black holes in 1916, with his general theory of relativity. This was always a theory that a lot of scientists and astrophysicists had considered, but it wasn't until 1971 that strong emissions of X-rays were detected from a point in the constellation Cygnus. The X-rays were thought to have originated from the first-ever detected black hole, though it was only confirmed 30 years after this time. [1] In 2019, an international array of coordinated telescopes, called the Event Horizon Telescope were able to achieve what used to be thought of as impossible: It captured an image a black hole. The distance this supermassive black hole was from us was about 53 million light years away. The mass of this black hole is about 6.5 billion stars that are the size of our sun. Black holes used to be thought of as this object of darkness that can never be captured as a picture since a black hole wont even let light escape. But black holes are too small and far away for regular telescopes to observe. The EHT array, however, is not a regular telescope; it's a collection of multiple millimeter-wavelength radio telescopes stationed at observatories from Antarctica to Greenland, Spain to Hawaii, and throughout the Americas. When their collective observations of a target object are synchronized, the EHT can achieve gathering images at a very high level - high enough to capture an image of a black hole. [2]
